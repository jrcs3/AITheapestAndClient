# The Liminal Hearth

## Meaning Behind the Name
The name shapes how clients experience the space and how Selene frames her work.
### Liminal
This reflects the in‑between states she specializes in:
- transitions
- identity shifts
- grief that doesn’t fit clean categories
- dreams that feel like messages
- emotional patterns that feel inherited or symbolic
Clients often arrive feeling like they’re standing on a threshold, unsure of what’s next. Selene uses tarot, dreamwork, and energy reading to help them understand what that threshold represents.
### Hearth
The hearth is the emotional anchor of the room. It represents:
- warmth
- safety
- ritual
- the sense of being held while doing difficult inner work
Selene keeps a small ceramic bowl with a candle at the center of her space, which she calls “the hearth flame.” It’s not ceremonial—just a quiet reminder that the room is meant to be a refuge.

### How the Name Shapes the Space
The Liminal Hearth feels like a place where the unconscious is allowed to speak.
- The altar shelf becomes the symbolic “hearth,” where clients place objects during sessions.
- The dream‑mapping wall reflects the liminal realm between waking and sleeping.
- The tarot table is the threshold where clients explore what they’re not yet ready to say directly.
- The lighting stays warm and low, reinforcing the hearth’s glow.
- The room’s layout encourages slow transitions—entering, settling, shifting, releasing.
Clients often say the name makes sense only after they’ve been there: it feels like stepping into a quiet doorway between worlds.


## Overall Layout and Atmosphere
Selene practices out of a converted second‑floor flat near Atwood Avenue, a space with creaking floorboards, slanted ceilings, and windows that catch the late‑afternoon light. The room is warm rather than mystical, with soft textures and natural materials that make people exhale as soon as they step inside. The air carries a faint mix of cedar, bergamot, and beeswax candles—never overpowering, just enough to signal that this is a place where the pace slows down.
The lighting is intentionally low and layered: salt lamps, small table lamps with amber bulbs, and a single string of warm fairy lights draped along a bookshelf. Nothing harsh, nothing fluorescent. The room feels like dusk even at noon.

## Objects and Tools That Shape the Space
Every object in the room has a purpose, whether symbolic, grounding, or simply comforting.
- A round table for tarot and symbolic work — The surface is smooth, dark wood, with a velvet cloth folded nearby. Selene keeps three decks on hand: one traditional, one mythic, one minimalist.
- A low altar shelf — Not religious, but symbolic: stones, feathers, dried herbs, a bowl of water, and a candle. Clients often place objects here during ritual release work.
- A dream‑mapping wall — A corkboard where clients can pin sketches or notes about recurring dreams, creating a visual map of their inner world.
- A woven rug from her mother — Faded indigo and cream, soft underfoot, grounding the room with family history.
- A cabinet of herbs and tinctures — Lavender, mugwort, chamomile, cedar, and blends she uses for scent-based grounding.
- A small brass singing bowl — Used sparingly, usually to mark the end of a session or to help a client settle after emotional work.
Nothing feels theatrical; everything feels lived‑in.

## Seating and Spatial Flow
Selene’s seating arrangement is designed to reduce hierarchy and increase emotional safety.
- Her chair is low-backed, upholstered in moss‑green fabric, angled slightly rather than directly facing the client.
- The client’s seat is a deep, cushioned armchair with a soft throw blanket draped over the side.
- A small table between them holds tissues, a ceramic bowl of smooth stones, and a carafe of water with two mismatched glasses.
The space encourages conversation that feels shared rather than interrogative.

## How the Room Functions During Sessions
The workspace shifts subtly depending on the client’s needs.
- For tarot or symbolic work, she clears the round table and invites the client to shuffle the deck.
- For energy work, she dims the lights further and has the client recline slightly, hands resting on their lap.
- For dream or narrative exploration, she uses the corkboard wall, pinning images or words as the client speaks.
- For ritual release, she opens a small window, lights a single candle, and guides the client through a symbolic letting‑go practice.
The room becomes a container—quiet, steady, and emotionally porous.

## Connection to Madison
Selene incorporates the city into her practice in subtle ways:
- She keeps lake stones on the altar, collected from the shores of Lake Monona.
- She burns locally made cedar bundles, a nod to both her upbringing and the region’s forests.
- She buys herbs from the Willy Street Co‑op and supports local artisans whose pottery and textiles fill the room.
- She often ends sessions by suggesting clients take a grounding walk along the Yahara River or through Olbrich Gardens.
Her workspace feels like Madison distilled into a single room—earthy, introspective, and quietly magical.
